Lecture 1: Types of Knowledge ▼

What Is Knowledge?

Research is often defined as the systematic exploration of problems through data collection and analysis. However, to understand research, we must first reflect on knowledge—its nature and forms.

Knowledge is not just facts in books. Drew (2020) identifies 14 types of knowledge, each serving unique roles in how we understand and interact with the world.

The 14 Types of Knowledge (Drew, 2020)

  1. A posteriori knowledge: Gained through experience (e.g., touching a hot stove).
  2. A priori knowledge: Independent of experience (e.g., mathematical truths like 1+1=2).
  3. Distributed knowledge: Spread across individuals (e.g., airplane design requiring experts in aerodynamics, propulsion, etc.).
  4. Domain/Expert knowledge: Specialized understanding within a field.
  5. Empirical knowledge: Derived from sensory observation or experimentation.
  6. Encoded knowledge: Stored in documents, databases, or formal systems.
  7. Tacit knowledge: Personal, intuitive, hard to articulate (e.g., riding a bike).
  8. Explicit knowledge: Codified, verbalizable, and easily shared.
  9. Metaknowledge: “Knowledge about knowledge”—knowing how to find or evaluate information.
  10. Procedural (Imperative) knowledge: “Knowing how” to perform tasks (e.g., cooking).
  11. Situated knowledge: Tied to specific contexts or cultures.
  12. Descriptive knowledge: Factual, neutral reporting of “what is.”
  13. Known unknowns: We know we don’t know—researchable gaps.
  14. Unknown unknowns: We don’t know we don’t know—beyond current imagination.

Lecture 2: Scientific Knowledge & Research Methodology ▼

What Makes Knowledge "Scientific"?

According to Bayley & Lucas (2007), scientific knowledge is "objectively proven because it is based on verifiable observation." It excludes belief, opinion, or speculation.

Science develops through a continuous cycle: theory → observation → refinement (Bhattacherjee, 2012). This process ensures reliability and objectivity.

Research, as defined by Singh (2006), means “to search again and again.” It requires a systematic procedure, openness to evidence, and rejection of preconceived opinions.

Lecture 3: Science – Institution, Process, and Result ▼

What Is Science?

Science (from Latin scientia = knowledge) is a systematic body of knowledge acquired through the scientific method (Bhattacherjee, 2012). It can be viewed from three perspectives:

  • As a social institution: Universities, labs, research centers, and scientific communities.
  • As a process: The methodology used to uncover knowledge.
  • As a result: The knowledge produced.

Key Characteristics of Science

Scientific inquiry must satisfy six core criteria (Berg, 2001):

  1. Objectivity: Absence of personal bias.
  2. Verifiability: Findings must be testable and demonstrable.
  3. Systematic exploration: Follows structured research steps.
  4. Precision and accuracy: Measurements must be exact and close to true values.
  5. Reliability: Consistent results over time and contexts.
  6. Ethicality: Honesty, integrity, proper attribution, and respect for subjects (Carpi & Egger, 2009).

Science vs. Pseudoscience

Not all disciplines are equally "scientific." Social sciences often face skepticism due to challenges in precision and replicability. However, Laugksch (2000) argues for scientific literacy—the ability to understand, apply, and evaluate scientific concepts—as the true benchmark, not the field itself.

Lecture 4: Research Problem, Questions, and Hypothesis ▼

Formulating a Research Problem

The research journey begins with a problem statement—a clear, unambiguous description of a gap, controversy, or deviation in existing knowledge (Thomas, 2015). A well-formulated problem determines the entire research trajectory.

According to Singh (2006), a good problem statement must:

  1. Involve a relationship between two or more variables.
  2. Be stated clearly in question form.
  3. Be amenable to empirical testing.

Selecting a Research Problem: Key Considerations (Kumar, 2011)

  • Interest: Sustains motivation.
  • Magnitude: Feasible in scope, time, and resources.
  • Measurement: Concepts must be operationalized (e.g., “happiness” → health, income, social ties).
  • Expertise: Prior knowledge helps identify gaps.
  • Data availability: Access to primary/secondary sources.
  • Relevance: Contribution to field or community.
  • Ethics: Legal, privacy, and dignity considerations.

Research Questions vs. Hypotheses

Research questions are open-ended, exploratory, and common in qualitative studies.

Hypotheses are predictive, testable statements about variable relationships, typical in quantitative research (Creswell, 2012).

Example Problem Statement

“Classrooms remain unchanged despite technological advances... This paper argues for integrating technology to create authentic learning contexts.”
