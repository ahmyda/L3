Lecture 1: Types of Knowledge ▼

What Is Knowledge?

At the very foundation of research lies the concept of knowledge. Research is often defined as the systematic exploration of problems through data collection and analysis—but to understand research, we must first understand knowledge itself.

Knowledge isn't just facts in books. It comes in many forms. The simplest distinction is between explicit knowledge (documented, like in databases) and tacit knowledge (personal, hard to express, like riding a bike). But according to Drew (2020), there are actually 14 types of knowledge, each with unique characteristics.

The 14 Types of Knowledge (Drew, 2020)

  1. A posteriori knowledge: Gained through experience (e.g., touching a hot stove)
  2. A priori knowledge: Independent of experience (e.g., 1+1=2)
  3. Distributed knowledge: Spread across many people (e.g., airplane design)
  4. Domain/Expert knowledge: Specialized understanding in a field
  5. Empirical knowledge: From sensory observation (research data)
  6. Encoded knowledge: Stored in documents, databases
  7. Tacit knowledge: Personal, intuitive, hard to formalize
  8. Explicit knowledge: Codified, verbalized, shareable
  9. Metaknowledge: "Knowledge about knowledge"—how to find information
  10. Procedural (Imperative) knowledge: "Knowing how" (e.g., cooking steps)
  11. Situated knowledge: Tied to specific context/culture
  12. Descriptive knowledge: Factual, neutral reporting
  13. Known unknowns: We know we don't know (researchable gaps)
  14. Unknown unknowns: We don't know we don't know (beyond imagination)

Worked Example

Example: Learning to Cook Pasta

Explicit knowledge: The recipe in a cookbook.
Tacit knowledge: Knowing when the pasta is "al dente" by feel.
Procedural knowledge: The sequence: boil water → add salt → cook pasta → drain.
Situated knowledge: Understanding that in high-altitude cities (e.g., Denver), water boils at a lower temperature, so pasta takes longer.
Metaknowledge: Knowing where to find authentic Italian recipes online.
A posteriori knowledge: Learning that overcooked pasta is mushy—by tasting it!

Comparison with Lecture 2

Knowledge vs. Scientific Knowledge

While Lecture 1 explores the diversity of knowledge, Lecture 2 focuses on what makes knowledge scientific. Not all 14 types qualify as scientific. For example, a dream (a posteriori) is not scientific because it can't be observed or verified by others. Scientific knowledge must be empirical, objective, and testable—filtering general knowledge through methodological rigor.

Model Essays (Exam Style)

Essay 1: "Tacit knowledge is essential in research." Discuss.

Introduction: While research demands explicit, measurable data, tacit knowledge—the intuitive, personal understanding gained through experience—plays a crucial yet often overlooked role in scholarly inquiry.

Body: Polanyi (1962) famously stated, "We know more than we can tell." In fields like education or medicine, professionals rely on tacit insights: a teacher sensing student confusion, or a surgeon's "feel" during an operation. Though difficult to quantify, this knowledge guides hypothesis generation, method design, and ethical judgment. Ignoring it risks creating sterile, context-free research. Reflective journals, peer observation, and narrative interviews can help make tacit dimensions visible.

Conclusion: The most impactful research integrates both explicit rigor and tacit wisdom. By acknowledging the role of intuition and experience, researchers produce work that is not only methodologically sound but also deeply human and applicable.

Essay 2: Can a dream be a source of scientific knowledge?

Introduction: Dreams have inspired scientific breakthroughs (e.g., Kekulé's benzene ring), but they cannot constitute scientific knowledge on their own.

Body: Scientific knowledge requires verifiability—others must observe and test it (Bayley & Lucas, 2007). A dream is a posteriori (you experienced it) but not empirical (not accessible to others' senses). However, if a dream sparks a testable idea—like a new theory of memory—and that idea survives experimentation, peer review, and replication, then it contributes indirectly to science. The dream is the seed; the scientific method is the soil.

Conclusion: Dreams are creative catalysts, not scientific evidence. Science begins not with inspiration alone, but with the disciplined testing of inspired ideas.

Lecture 2: Scientific Knowledge & Research Methodology ▼

What Makes Knowledge "Scientific"?

Not all knowledge is scientific. According to Bayley & Lucas (2007), scientific knowledge is "objectively proven because it is based on verifiable observation." This means it must come from what can be seen, heard, touched, or measured—not from opinion, belief, or speculation.

Scientific knowledge is built through a continuous cycle: theory → observation → refinement (Bhattacherjee, 2012). Researchers propose explanations (theories), test them with data, and revise based on evidence. Over time, this process creates reliable, objective knowledge.

Worked Example

Example: Testing a New Teaching Method

Non-scientific approach: "My students seem more engaged since I started group work!" (subjective)
Scientific approach:
- Theory: "Structured group work improves writing scores."
- Method: Randomly assign two similar classes—one uses group work, one doesn't.
- Data: Use the same rubric to score essays before and after.
- Analysis: Compare score changes statistically.
- Verification: Publish methods so others can replicate.
Only the second approach produces scientific knowledge.

Comparison with Lecture 1

General Knowledge vs. Scientific Knowledge

Lecture 1 shows knowledge can be personal, cultural, or intuitive. Lecture 2 adds the critical filter of scientific validation. Your belief that "music helps focus" is valid personal knowledge—but becomes scientific only after controlled testing, peer review, and replication. Science doesn't reject other knowledge types; it refines them through method.

Model Essays (Exam Style)

Essay 1: "All intelligent human behavior involves some research." Evaluate.

Introduction: Singh (2006) cites Rusk: "All intelligent human behavior involves some research." This broad view expands research beyond academia into daily problem-solving.

Body: Choosing a phone involves comparing features (data collection); navigating a new city uses observation and adjustment. These mirror research's core: identifying problems and seeking evidence-based solutions. However, academic research adds rigor: systematic design, bias control, and ethical standards. Everyday "research" may lack these, risking flawed conclusions (e.g., confirmation bias in news consumption).

Conclusion: The statement is insightful but incomplete. While all intelligent action involves inquiry, only disciplined methodology transforms it into reliable knowledge. Science elevates natural curiosity into collective understanding.

Essay 2: Why must scientific knowledge be falsifiable?

Introduction: Falsifiability—the ability to disprove a claim—is a cornerstone of science (Bhattacherjee, 2012). Without it, knowledge becomes dogma.

Body: A theory like "All swans are white" is scientific because finding one black swan falsifies it. In contrast, "Astrology influences mood" is unfalsifiable—any outcome can be explained post-hoc. Falsifiability forces precision: theories must make clear, testable predictions. It enables self-correction, distinguishing science from pseudoscience.

Conclusion: Falsifiability ensures science remains evidence-based, not belief-based. It is not a weakness but science's greatest strength—allowing error to become progress.

Lecture 3: Science ▼

What Is Science?

Science is a systematic body of knowledge acquired through the scientific method (Bhattacherjee, 2012). It can be viewed from three perspectives:

  • As a social institution: Universities, labs, journals, peer review
  • As a process: Research methodology—the cycle of theory and observation
  • As a result: The accumulated, verified knowledge itself

For any inquiry to be scientific, it must satisfy four criteria: replicability, precision, falsifiability, and parsimony.

The Four Criteria of Science

  1. Replicability: Others must repeat your study and get similar results.
  2. Precision: Measurements must be exact and clearly defined.
  3. Falsifiability: Theories must be testable and potentially disprovable.
  4. Parsimony: Prefer the simplest explanation (Occam's Razor).

Additionally, science must be objective, verifiable, systematic, reliable, accurate, and ethical (Berg, 2001).

Worked Example

Example: Climate Change Research

Replicability: Global teams use same satellite data and models.
Precision: CO₂ measured in ppm; temperature in 0.01°C increments.
Falsifiability: "If CO₂ rises, global temps rise" → testable prediction.
Parsimony: Explains warming via greenhouse effect, not complex conspiracies.
Ethics: Data openly shared; conflicts of interest disclosed.

Comparison with Lecture 2

Scientific Knowledge vs. Science

Lecture 2 defines scientific knowledge as the output of research. Lecture 3 expands this by examining science as the entire ecosystem—including institutions (universities), processes (methodology), and ethical norms. Knowledge is the product; science is the factory, the workers, and the quality control.

Model Essays (Exam Style)

Essay 1: "There is no failure in scientific research if procedures are carefully carried out." Discuss.

Introduction: This statement reframes "failure" in science—not as negative results, but as methodological shortcuts.

Body: A well-designed study that finds "no effect" is invaluable: it prevents wasted effort, refines theories, and combats publication bias. True failure occurs when ethics are breached, methods are sloppy, or data is manipulated to fit expectations. As Allington & Swann (2009) note, ethical process outweighs desired outcomes.

Conclusion: Science advances through honest inquiry, not guaranteed success. Rigorous process—regardless of result—is the hallmark of scientific integrity.

Essay 2: Are social sciences "real" science?

Introduction: Critics argue social sciences lack the precision of physics, labeling them "pseudoscience." This misrepresents scientific criteria.

Body: While controlled experiments are harder in sociology, social sciences adhere to core principles: systematic methods, falsifiable hypotheses, peer review, and ethics. Laugksch (2000) advocates "scientific literacy"—evaluating fields by their methodology, not just numerical precision. Psychology's replication projects exemplify self-correction, a scientific virtue.

Conclusion: Science is defined by process, not subject matter. Social sciences meet this standard, enriching our understanding of human systems with rigor and humility.

Lecture 4: Research Problem, Questions, and Hypothesis ▼

Formulating a Research Problem

The most crucial step in research is defining a clear problem (Thomas, 2015). A good problem statement must:

  1. Address relationships between two or more variables
  2. Be stated clearly as a question
  3. Be amenable to empirical testing (Singh, 2006)

Consider these 7 factors when selecting a topic (Kumar, 2011): Interest, Magnitude, Measurement, Expertise, Relevance, Data Availability, Ethics.

Research Questions vs. Hypotheses

Research questions are open-ended and exploratory—ideal for qualitative studies.
Hypotheses are predictive statements about variable relationships—used in quantitative research (Creswell, 2012).

Example:
Question: "How do students experience online learning?"
Hypothesis: "Students with high self-efficacy report higher satisfaction in online courses."

Worked Example

Example: Technology in Classrooms

Problem: Despite tech advances, classrooms remain unchanged, causing student disengagement.
Research Question: "How does integrating interactive technology affect engagement in secondary math?"
Hypothesis: "Students using interactive simulations will show 20% higher participation than those in traditional lectures."
Variables: Tech integration (independent), engagement (dependent, measured via observation + surveys).

Comparison with Lecture 3

Science as Process vs. Research Design

Lecture 3 describes science's broad methodology. Lecture 4 zooms into the starting point: how to formulate a problem that aligns with scientific criteria. A well-crafted problem ensures the entire research process (Lecture 3) remains focused, ethical, and replicable.

Model Essays (Exam Style)

Essay 1: Why is problem formulation the most crucial research step?

Introduction: Thomas (2015) states, "A large part of the problem lies in knowing what one is trying to do." Problem formulation sets the research trajectory.

Body: The problem determines methodology, sample, tools, and analysis. A vague problem ("improve education") yields unfocused data. A precise one ("Does peer feedback improve essay quality in EFL learners?") enables targeted design. It also ensures ethical alignment and feasibility (Kumar, 2011).

Conclusion: Investing time in problem formulation prevents wasted resources and strengthens validity. It transforms curiosity into scientific inquiry.

Essay 2: When should a researcher use a hypothesis instead of a research question?

Introduction: The choice depends on research paradigm, goals, and existing knowledge.

Body: Use hypotheses in quantitative, deductive research with strong theoretical foundations (e.g., testing a drug's efficacy). Use research questions in qualitative, exploratory studies where relationships are unknown (e.g., understanding refugee experiences). Mixed methods may use both: questions for exploration, hypotheses for validation.

Conclusion: Neither is superior—they serve different purposes. Alignment with epistemology and research goals is key.
